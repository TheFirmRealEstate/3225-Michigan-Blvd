3225 Michigan Blvd – Architectural Significance

Exploring the Art Deco heritage, architectural innovation, and enduring legacy of Racine’s lakefront landmark.

Perched above the shoreline of Lake Michigan, 3225 Michigan Blvd stands as one of Racine’s most architecturally distinctive private residences. Completed in the early 1930s, the home embodies the optimism and design ambition of the interwar Midwest: a carefully balanced composition of horizontal lines, rounded corners, and refined decorative detail that together express an elegant Art Deco modernism.

Built for a prosperous local executive, the house was conceived to take full advantage of its lakefront siting. Large windows frame broad views across the water while terraces and low parapets reinforce a strong relationship between the building and the shoreline. The design intentionally balances monumentality with domestic scale — a hallmark of Edwin J. Krause’s residential work.

Edwin J. Krause was a regional architect whose work bridged traditional craftsmanship and the emerging modernist vocabulary of the early twentieth century. In Racine and Milwaukee, Krause’s commissions ranged from commercial storefronts to private residences, always emphasizing clarity of form and attention to detail.

At 3225 Michigan Blvd, Krause employed the Art Deco emphasis on geometry and rhythm without resorting to excessive ornament. The façade’s brickwork, metal windows, and carefully proportioned bays demonstrate an economy of means paired with precise craftsmanship — a modern approach intended to convey both refinement and durability.

The house was commissioned by Elmer Voigt, a senior executive at Western Printing and Lithographing Company. Voigt’s professional ties to the printing and lithographic industries are visible in subtle ways throughout the home — from the careful patterning of stonework to decorative motifs that recall printed surfaces.

One of the residence’s most unusual features is a set of printed or lithographically decorated stone tiles in the lower level. These decorative tiles blend technical skill with aesthetic restraint and may well reflect a cross-pollination of ideas between Voigt’s professional life and the domestic environment he commissioned.

The residence blends functional planning with decorative restraint: horizontal banding and rounded corners soften the façade, while bespoke metalwork, cabinetry, and integrated mechanical systems demonstrate an early commitment to modern living standards.

Interior spaces were planned for comfort and gracious entertaining. Careful material choices — from tile and terrazzo to brass fittings — underscore an approach that values both durability and refined detailing. The house’s staircase is a particularly striking example of form meeting craft: a flowing, curvilinear composition that anchors the interior circulation.

In the 1930s Racine was a city of invention and industry. Companies such as Western Publishing, J.I. Case, and Modine Manufacturing helped create a robust local economy that supported sophisticated residential architecture and civic patronage.

3225 Michigan Blvd participates in this local narrative: it is both a personal residence and a statement about the city’s confidence in design and craftsmanship. Nearby landmarks — including works by Frank Lloyd Wright and other regional architects — provide a broader context in which this house can be read as part of the city’s architectural lineage.

Today, 3225 Michigan Blvd remains a living piece of Racine’s architectural legacy. Successive owners have respected the original design while updating systems and finishes where necessary to meet contemporary expectations without compromising historic character.

Preservation efforts have focused on retaining original fabric where possible: brick and masonry repairs have been completed using compatible materials, windows have been carefully restored, and interior features such as tile and metalwork have been conserved. The home continues to function as a private residence while also serving as an example of regional Art Deco residential design.
