The Signed Hearth of 3225 Michigan Boulevard

Where the Art of Printing Met the Art of Homebuilding

You may be wondering what you’re looking at. This fireplace isn’t made of hand-painted or glazed ceramic tiles — it’s lithographic printing on stone, built right into the architecture of the home. The names you see weren’t scrawled in ink and fired in a kiln; they were transferred through a meticulous printing process, the same kind that gave Racine its world-class reputation for fine lithography.

From the day this house was built, the hearth stood like a printed page in three dimensions — part art, part industry, part declaration.

In the early 20th century, Racine was one of the great printing capitals of America. Western Printing & Lithographing Co., just blocks away, produced everything from classic children’s books to art prints and decorative panels.

This fireplace belongs to that legacy. The same techniques used to print storybooks, maps, and advertisements were applied here to stone — proof that Western’s craftsmen didn’t just print on paper. They experimented with surfaces, processes, and permanence.

Every signature surrounding the hearth was printed, not handwritten directly on the tile. It’s a composition of many hands — perhaps employees, artists, and family members of Western Printing. Names like Voigt, Homburg, Sheets, Anderson, and Kraus appear — some tied to Western Printing’s early leadership, others to Racine’s artistic and industrial community.

Their signatures overlap and swirl across the tiles, like the credits of a film etched into the very heart of a home.

Why a fireplace? Because it’s where warmth and permanence meet — the perfect canvas for a city built on making things that last. This wasn’t a private flourish or hidden time capsule. It was always meant to be seen. The homeowners — and perhaps Western Printing itself — wanted to make a statement: that craftsmanship, design, and pride belong not just in galleries or books, but in everyday life.

Lithographic transfer on stone or tile required extraordinary precision. Artists wrote in ink on a flat litho plate, which was then chemically treated and pressed onto the surface. It’s the same principle behind Western’s printed books — only here, it’s architectural. The subtle variations, the natural texture of the tiles, and the clarity of the signatures all suggest this was more than decoration: a demonstration of mastery, perhaps even a showpiece for clients or visiting designers.

Nearly a century later, this fireplace still speaks to the spirit of the city that made it — inventive, collaborative, proud. It’s not just a record of names. It’s a printed artifact — a physical link between Racine’s industrial innovation and its artistic soul. In its quiet way, it says what every printer, craftsman, and builder once believed: that good work deserves to be signed, and beautiful work deserves to endure.
