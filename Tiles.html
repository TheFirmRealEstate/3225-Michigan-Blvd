3225 Michigan Blvd

The Printed Stone Tiles

A quick tour of the mysterious lower‑level tiles, their Art Deco heritage, and the Western Publishing connection.

Step into the lower level and the floor immediately steals the show. At first glance it reads as natural stone; look closer and you’ll notice the veining and textures are printed. These tiles likely use a lithographic or enamel printing process applied to a stone, terrazzo, or quarry‑tile base—an innovative technique that blends art with engineering.

Close-up showing printed veining and subtle color variation.

3225 Michigan Blvd was commissioned by Elmer Voigt, an executive at Western Printing and Lithographing Company—the famed creators behind popular children's books and printed ephemera in the region.

Tile field near the stair—note the subtle layout and repeat pattern.

Designed in the early 1930s, the home reflects Racine’s Art Deco optimism—crisp geometry, modern materials, and exquisite detail. The printed tiles echo that spirit: elegant, experimental, and well executed.

Edge detail and grout—installation quality that has held up remarkably well.

Every surface here speaks to Racine’s golden age of invention. These tiles connect the home to the city’s creative industries—where publishing, machinery, and design cross‑pollinated.

View across the lower-level floor—scale and rhythm of the pattern in context.

Treat the tiles as you would any historic surface: mild cleaners, soft pads, and periodic sealing when appropriate. Their significance is more than aesthetic—they are a tangible link between the home’s design and Racine’s industrial artistry.
